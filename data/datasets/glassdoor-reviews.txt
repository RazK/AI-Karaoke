Management here operates like a high school clique where the cool kids get promotions and everyone else gets gaslighted into thinking they're not working hard enough. I watched three people with half my experience leapfrog me because they played golf with the VP on weekends. When I brought it up to HR, they literally told me to "focus on my own journey." My journey led me straight to the exit.

They love to brag about their "unlimited PTO" policy in interviews, but the second you actually try to take a vacation, your manager hits you with passive-aggressive Slack messages about "optics" and "team commitment." I once got a negative performance review partly because I took two weeks off in a year. Two weeks. In a country where that's already pathetically little. The policy exists purely as a recruitment trap.

The CEO does a monthly all-hands where he talks for ninety minutes about his "vision" while half the company is quietly updating their resumes on a second monitor. Last quarter he announced a company-wide pay freeze while simultaneously renovating his office with what I can only describe as a boutique hotel aesthetic. Marble countertops in a man's personal office while engineers share monitors. The disconnect would be funny if it weren't so insulting.

I have never seen a company burn through new hires faster. They dangle exciting projects during interviews, then park you on maintenance work for a legacy system nobody cares about. The onboarding is a three-hour video playlist from 2019 and a pat on the back. Most people figure out the bait-and-switch within six weeks, and the ones who stay are just too tired to job hunt again.

If you enjoy being micromanaged to the point where you need to justify a fifteen-minute bathroom break in your time-tracking software, this is the place for you. My team lead once pulled me into a meeting to discuss why my mouse showed me as "idle" on Teams for eleven minutes on a Tuesday. I was eating lunch. At my desk. While reading a work document that apparently didn't generate enough clicks to satisfy the surveillance software.

The "collaborative culture" they advertise is actually just a system where nobody is empowered to make a single decision without seven layers of approval. I once needed sign-off from four directors to change the color of a button on an internal tool that maybe twelve people use. Projects that should take a week take three months, and then leadership wonders why we're behind every competitor in the market. Innovation doesn't die here — it's smothered slowly with a pillow made of process documents.

They laid off thirty percent of the workforce two days before Christmas and had the audacity to frame it as "right-sizing for future growth" in a company-wide email that ended with "we remain a family." The people who were let go had to box up their desks while a security guard watched. Some of them had been there over a decade. But hey, the C-suite still got their bonuses in January, so I guess the family analogy tracks if your family is the Lannisters.

Work-life balance is treated as a personal failing here. I was once praised in a meeting for answering an email at eleven pm on a Saturday, and I realized that was the moment my soul left my body. The people who get ahead are the ones who make performative suffering their entire personality. I started therapy three months into this job and my therapist eventually said, and I quote, "have you considered that your workplace might be the problem?"

Diversity and inclusion here is a page on the website and absolutely nothing else. The leadership team is twelve white men named either Mike, Dave, or Chris, and they once hired a "Head of DEI" who reported to nobody, had no budget, and quit after five months. When she left, they didn't replace her and nobody mentioned it again. They still use her photo in recruiting materials though, which tells you everything you need to know.

The tech stack is held together with duct tape and prayers. We were running critical production systems on infrastructure so outdated that our vendor literally stopped supporting it two years ago. Every time engineering raised the issue, leadership said it was "on the roadmap," which I eventually learned is corporate for "we will never fund this." When things inevitably broke, it was always the engineers who got blamed for not being creative enough with the digital equivalent of a rotary phone.
